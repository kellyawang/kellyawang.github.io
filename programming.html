Interactive Code Art

This interactive, computational art installation was generated by custom software written in Processing. A webcam sitting atop the screen captures a continuous video feed, which is processed by a facial recognition algorithm. Found faces are tracked and mapped in real time to a 2D array of pixels, and translated to a reactive video display.

SPLIT

May 2016
Exhibited in a gallery show at the Williams College Museum of Art.
See the source code on Github.






Brainstorming Process:

Drawing inspiration from the work of Camille Utterback and Theodore Watson, and Petros Vrellis' interactive Starry Night app.

I started out with some early sketches to work out the facial recognition and tracking. I experimented with reactive pattern-making and different abstract shapes and colors:

I was increasingly drawn to the overlapping, abstract shapes and the assertive, sharp edges of the triangles. In the final design, the long, blade-like triangles lance across the screen towards found faces (denoted by the green circles). As people move laterally through the space in front of the screen, the blades hastily reorient themselves.


Computer Generated Film

All films were made during the CS371: Computational Graphics course at Williams College in the Fall of 2014. Videos were created and rendered using the open-source C++ G3D Innovation Engine with original cinematography and artistic design and open-source music.

Illumination

October 2014
Special honors: Ward Prize for Best Project in Computer Science (2015)
Click here for presentation slides.
For best results please watch in HD



This short film features a procedurally generated city illuminated by neon lights designed, modeled, and rendered as part of a midterm group project. I was responsible for modeling and creating 3D models that were used as primitives in an original grammar that generated the buildings. I also led the team in developing a unified artistic vision that carried through to the final film.
Our group made strategic decisions for the two week span of the project in deciding to split up into teams that focused on creating the basic primitives, generating each building, generating an entire city, and writing scripts and an interface to manage the entire process. Because each building file was so massive, we needed to strategize when we were defining spline curves for the making of the video, and thus created a method for defining splines in a proxy city made up of simple rectangles, and then transferring the camera movements to the real city. Having responsive and dedicated team members allowed us to recognize when we needed to scale down our efforts in certain areas, and how to stay on track and complete each component of the project in a timely manner.

Steel Skull

October 2014
Special honors: Won "Best Animated Film" in Williams College Movie Madness Contest 2016
Click here for full project documentation



This short film was created for a lesson on rendering implicit surfaces using GLSL shaders I wrote and ran on the GPU. The skull I created piece by piece as a whimsical response to the prompt. Each shot is composed with attention to camera angles and varying tempo of both subject and camera movement.



Color Rush Game Trailer

November 2014
Click here for week 1 documentation (gameplay) and week 2 documentation.



This is a short trailer created for an infinite runner game I programmed in G3D. The player is a pink polyhedron rushing towards the end of the road to upbeat music and picking up as many points as possible along the way. I composed the visual environment of the game and worked especially on refining the player experience. Week 1 was spent implementing user interaction with placeholder game pieces. Week 2 was spent completing implementation and finalizing design.
